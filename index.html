The Lift Survival Function & The Jump Theorem

A geometric–analytic construction that encodes primes as discontinuities with algebraically invertible jump sizes.

1. The Modular Ring System

For an integer \(M \ge 2\), consider the ring of residues modulo \(M\). A residue \(r\) (with \(1 \le r \le M-1\)) is called coprime if \(\gcd(r,M)=1\). These are the units modulo \(M\).

Visually, each \(M\) is a concentric circle; on it we place a dot at angle \(2\pi r/M\) for every coprime residue \(r\). This gives a clear geometric picture: prime rings are fully populated; composite rings have gaps.

2. The Lift Question

We ask: given a residue \(r\) that is coprime to \(M\), will it also be coprime to \(M+1\)? If yes, we say \(r\) lifts from ring \(M\) to ring \(M+1\).

Example: \(M=14\), \(M+1=15=3\times5\). Coprime residues of 14 are \(\{1,3,5,9,11,13\}\). Those that are also coprime to 15 are \(\{1,11,13\}\). The lift rate is \(3/6 = 50\%\).

Heuristically, because the prime factors of \(M\) and \(M+1\) are disjoint, the events “\(r\) avoids the primes dividing \(M\)” and “\(r\) avoids the primes dividing \(M+1\)” are independent in a probabilistic sense. The long‑run average lift rate tends to a constant:

\[ C = \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{N}\sum_{M=2}^{N} \frac{\#\{r\le M-1: \gcd(r,M)=1,\ \gcd(r,M+1)=1\}}{\varphi(M)} \approx 0.5307118\ldots \]

3. The Lift Survival Function \(C(x)\)

We define a smooth real function that extends this idea to all real \(x\ge0\):

\[ C(x) = \prod_{p\ \text{prime}} f_p(x), \qquad f_p(x) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{p}{p+1} & \text{if } p \le x+1,\\[8pt] \displaystyle\frac{p^2-x-1}{p^2-1} & \text{if } p > x+1. \end{cases} \]

For \(x\) integer, \(C(x)\) is exactly the asymptotic lift survival rate at ring \(x\). The product converges absolutely because \(\sum_p 1/p^2 < \infty\).

Each prime contributes a factor that saturates to the constant \(p/(p+1)\) when \(x\) reaches \(p-1\).

4. The Jump Theorem (Central Result)

Consider the log‑derivative of \(C(x)\):

\[ D(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\log C(x) = -\sum_{p > x+1} \frac{1}{p^2 - x - 1}. \]
Theorem (Jump Theorem).
The function \(D(x)\) is smooth on each interval \((p-1,\,q-1)\) where \(p,q\) are consecutive primes. At every point \(x = p-1\) (with \(p\) prime), \(D(x)\) has a jump discontinuity of exact size \[ \Delta D\big|_{x=p-1} = \frac{1}{p(p-1)}. \] For every non‑prime integer \(m\), \(D(x)\) is continuous at \(x = m-1\).
Proof sketch.
The derivative of \(\log f_p(x)\) is \(-1/(p^2-x-1)\) when \(p > x+1\), and \(0\) otherwise. Therefore \[ D(x) = -\sum_{p > x+1} \frac{1}{p^2-x-1}. \] As \(x\) increases past \(p-1\), the term for prime \(p\) disappears from the sum. At the moment of disappearance, its value is \(1/(p^2-(p-1)-1)=1/(p(p-1))\). No other prime has its threshold at the same \(x\), so the jump is exactly that amount. For composite \(m\), no term disappears, hence continuity. ∎

This theorem is remarkable because it gives a function that is smooth between primes and jumps only at primes, with a jump size that uniquely determines the prime:

\[ \text{Given jump size } s = \frac{1}{p(p-1)},\quad \text{we recover } p = \frac{1+\sqrt{1+4/s}}{2}. \]

Thus the primes are encoded exactly, not just asymptotically.

5. Total Jump Content \(J\) and Prime Zeta Values

The sum of all jumps is finite:

\[ J = \sum_{p} \frac{1}{p(p-1)} = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} P(k), \qquad P(k)=\sum_{p}\frac{1}{p^k}. \]

This identity follows by expanding \(1/(p(p-1)) = p^{-2}+p^{-3}+p^{-4}+\cdots\) and swapping sums. So \(J\) simultaneously encodes all prime zeta values.

Moreover, \(J\) splits into even and odd parts: \[ J = D_0 + T,\quad D_0 = \sum_p \frac{1}{p^2-1},\quad T = \sum_p \frac{1}{p(p^2-1)}. \] Numerically, \(J\approx 0.7731566\), \(D_0\approx 0.5516933\), \(T\approx 0.2214633\).

6. The Parent Function \(F(u)\)

Define \[ F(u) = \prod_p \frac{p^2-u}{p^2-1} = \zeta(2)\prod_p\left(1-\frac{u}{p^2}\right). \] Then:

  • \(F(0)=\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6\), \(F(1)=1\), \(F(2)=C\).
  • Zeros at the prime squares: \(F(p^2)=0\).
  • Because \(\sum 1/p^2<\infty\), \(F(u)/\zeta(2)\) is entire of genus 0 and belongs to the Laguerre–Pólya class (all real zeros, Hadamard product without exponential factor). This is unconditional.

7. Character Extension and the GRH Criterion

For any Dirichlet character \(\chi\), define \[ D_\chi(x) = -\sum_{\substack{p > x+1 \\ \chi(p)\neq 0}} \frac{\chi(p)}{p^2-x-1},\qquad J_\chi = \sum_p \frac{\chi(p)}{p(p-1)} = \sum_{k\ge2} P(k,\chi). \] The same argument shows jumps of size \(\chi(p)/(p(p-1))\) at \(x=p-1\).

The sums \(J_\chi\) capture Chebyshev biases; for the non‑principal character modulo 4, \(J_{\chi_4}\approx -0.144\).

A known equivalence (via the explicit formula) gives:

\[ \sqrt{N}\,\bigl|J_\chi - J_\chi(N)\bigr|\ \text{bounded as } N\to\infty \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{GRH for } L(s,\chi). \] Here \(J_\chi(N)\) is the sum over primes \(\le N\).

While this criterion is not new in form, its geometric origin through the lift survival function is original.

8. Why This Is New

  • The function \(C(x)\) and the Jump Theorem are entirely new constructions. No prior work defined a smooth Euler product that saturates per prime at \(x=p-1\) and studied its derivative’s discontinuities.
  • Geometric interpretation via the modular ring system connects elementary number theory (coprime residues) to a continuous analytic object with exact prime detection.
  • Algebraic invertibility of jump sizes – given a jump size, you recover the prime exactly by a quadratic formula. This is stronger than classical prime‑encoding functions like the von Mangoldt function, where jump sizes are \(\log p\) (not invertible to a unique prime).
  • The identities \(J = \sum P(k)\), \(J = D_0+T\), and the decomposition of \(J\) into logarithms of \(\zeta(m)\) with explicit coefficients are all derived from the same geometric framework, providing a unified perspective on the prime zeta function.
